Research

Shaping tomorrow every day

At the Luddy School of Informatics, Computing, and Engineering, we see what tomorrow can be and we make it reality.

Our research covers a broad range of disciplines, from the highly technical to the highly social. Every day, our faculty work across these disciplines to advance and improve today’s tech-driven world, often with teams that include students. Our commitment to research encompasses the study of how students learn with faculty committed to the practice of teaching and learning as a scholarly pursuit.

Four research pillars

Luddy’s research pillars are built on a foundation of security and privacy, with consideration of the ethical implications of technology. These pillars support a wide variety of applications, including improving trust in the legitimacy of digital information, creating systems to improve health and well-being, and helping people enhance their lives through better decision making.

Human-centered computing

Human-centered computing (HCC) is the convergence of multiple disciplines to better design and understand technologies that support human activities. Luddy researchers’ focus areas range from how computing affects culture and society to the development of tools and systems that empower people to better understand, manage, and improve their health and environment.

Research areas include:

  • Computing, culture, and society
  • Digital humanities
  • Human computer interaction
  • Human-computer interaction design (HCI/d)
  • Proactive health informatics

Sensors

The internet of things (IoT) results in billions of sensors embedded in the world, continuously monitoring people, technologies, and our environment. Luddy research has a significant focus on devices that detect, measure, record, and respond to an environment or physical condition. Our research encompasses the entire range of sensor technology development.

Research areas include:

  • Engineering health
  • Human-computer interaction design (HCI/d)
  • Intelligent computer engineering
  • Proactive health informatics

Data infrastructure

Data infrastructure refers to the basic physical and organizational structures and facilities needed to operate a digital pathway, including the protection, preservation, processing, delivery, and security of data, as well as the applications for information services delivery.

Research areas include:

  • Data science
  • Information and library science
  • Intelligent computer engineering
  • Programming languages

Data analytics

The qualitative and quantitative techniques and processes used to extract knowledge from data enable organizations to make more informed decisions, and enable researchers to verify or disprove scientific models, theories, and hypotheses.

Research areas include:

  • Artificial intelligence
  • Bioinformatics and computational biology
  • Complex networks and systems
  • Machine learning
  • Theoretical computer science

Students, join a research project

Whether you’re a first-year undergrad or a Ph.D. student, we have a research project for you. Research is a central component of some academic programs, like doctorates. For other programs, it’s a way for you to get valuable experience, find mentors, and show future employers or graduate programs what you can do.

We also host non-Luddy students for summer research programs.

Contact the Office of the Associate Dean for Research

The Office of the Associate Dean for Research is the research gateway for Luddy faculty. We help faculty collaborate, access resources, overcome obstacles, and achieve their research goals.

For assistance with research collaborations, contact Kay Connelly.

For nontechnical research needs, such as funding opportunities and grant management, contact Pantea Pishgahi.
